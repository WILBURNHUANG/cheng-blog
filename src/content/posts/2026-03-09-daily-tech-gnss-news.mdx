## Tech News

### Anthropic Sues to Block Pentagon Blacklisting

Anthropic filed a lawsuit on Sunday to block the Pentagon from placing it on a national security blacklist, escalating the AI company's standoff with the Defense Department. The suit argues that the supply-chain risk designation—a label typically reserved for foreign adversaries—is unlawful and violates Anthropic's free speech and due process rights.

The confrontation stems from Anthropic's refusal to grant the military unrestricted access to its Claude AI models. The company insisted the Defense Department affirm it would not use Claude for domestic mass surveillance or fully autonomous weapons. Anthropic had a **$200 million** contract with the military before the designation.

In a remarkable development, Anthropic's consumer adoption has surged amid the controversy. Claude reached **#1** on the U.S. Apple App Store last week, dethroning ChatGPT for the first time. ChatGPT uninstalls jumped **295%** day-over-day as a grassroots "QuitGPT" movement gathered **1.5 million** participants and held a physical protest outside OpenAI's San Francisco headquarters.

### Oracle and OpenAI Cancel Texas Data Center Expansion

Oracle Corp. and OpenAI have scrapped plans to expand a flagship AI data center in Abilene, Texas, after negotiations dragged over financing and OpenAI's changing needs. The collapsed talks created an opening for Meta Platforms Inc. to step in and consider leasing the planned expansion site.

The cancellation comes amid growing scrutiny of the **$700 billion** AI data center boom and its impact on energy costs. President Trump recently convened tech leaders at the White House to sign a pledge that they will supply their own power for AI data centers, as electricity prices are forecast to rise **6%** through 2026 and another **3%** in 2028 according to Goldman Sachs.

NVIDIA, meanwhile, announced a **$2 billion** investment in Lumentum to advance data center optics technology, while Hyundai Motor Group committed **$6.3 billion** to establish an AI data center and innovation hub in South Korea.

### Apple Launches Seven New Products in March Blitz

Apple delivered on CEO Tim Cook's promise of "a big week ahead" with a flurry of new product announcements. The standout is the **$599** MacBook Neo, the company's most affordable laptop ever, featuring the A18 Pro chip in four colors with **60%** recycled content—the highest percentage of any Apple product.

Other launches include the **iPhone 17e** starting at **$599** with doubled storage (256GB), MagSafe, and a new C1X modem; the **iPad Air with M4** promising **30%** faster performance; and the **MacBook Air with M5** now starting with **512GB** storage at **$1,099**. Apple also refreshed its Studio Display lineup with Thunderbolt 5 connectivity and introduced a new Studio Display XDR.

The refresh comes as Apple takes a notably different approach to AI than its Big Tech peers. While competitors announced **$115 billion to $200 billion** capex plans for 2026, Apple returned **$24.7 billion** to shareholders via buybacks in a single quarter—a strategy the market has rewarded.

### OpenClaw Fever Sweeps China as Tech Giants Race to Adopt

Open-source AI agent software OpenClaw has become a cultural phenomenon in China, with nearly **1,000 people** lining up outside Tencent's Shenzhen headquarters on Friday to have engineers install the software on their computers for free. The crowd included amateur developers, retired engineers, housewives, students, and AI enthusiasts.

Known colloquially as "Little Lobster" (小龙虾) in China, OpenClaw—built by Austrian engineer Peter Steinberger—took roughly **100 days** to become the most-starred repository in GitHub history. Chinese consumers are using it for stock picking, report writing, slide decks, emails, and coding tasks.

China's biggest cloud firms have moved aggressively to capitalize on the trend. Tencent, Alibaba, ByteDance, JD.com, and Baidu have all launched competing free OpenClaw installation campaigns, while each has also launched services running OpenClaw on their cloud platforms—something none of the U.S. cloud giants has done. The Shenzhen Longgang District AI Bureau even released draft policy proposing government support for OpenClaw adoption. Related software stocks including UCloud Technology, QingCloud Technologies, and Hangzhou Shunwang Technology all surged around **20%** on Monday.

## GNSS News

### Topcon and GSSI Announce GPR-GNSS Integration

Topcon Positioning Systems and Geophysical Survey Systems, Inc. (GSSI) announced a collaboration to pair GSSI's advanced ground penetrating radar systems with Topcon's GNSS solutions and mass data workflow software. The integrated solution will support subsurface intelligence applications across infrastructure and construction projects.

This partnership adds to Topcon's expanded ecosystem unveiled at CONEXPO-CON/AGG 2026, which includes the **Hybrid Lock** feature in 3D-MC that automatically switches between robotic total station tracking and GNSS to maintain continuous machine guidance. Topcon also announced a collaboration with Fixposition to address positioning challenges in dense urban environments where satellite connection is unreliable.

Additionally, Xwatch Safety Solutions (part of Hexagon) announced its integration with Topcon's MC-X Platform, creating a connected layer between machine control and on-machine safety for mixed construction fleets.

### Trimble Releases Firmware v6.43 with Expanded Tilt Compensation

Trimble introduced GNSS firmware version 6.43 for its latest generation of survey and positioning receivers, delivering significant accuracy improvements and enhanced cybersecurity protections. The update expands the allowable pole tilt angle for Trimble TIP tilt compensation technology from **30 degrees** to **40 degrees**, giving surveyors greater operational flexibility.

The firmware also supports the expansion of Trimble CenterPoint RTX Fast services through a new regional correction network covering **South Korea**. CenterPoint RTX Fast provides centimeter-level positioning accuracy without the need for a local RTK base station, making it particularly valuable for precision agriculture and construction applications in the region.
