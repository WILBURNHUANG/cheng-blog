Monday's tape had two stories running in opposite directions. In the real economy, the AI buildout kept writing enormous checks—a $9.8 billion data center lease, a $188 billion data-platform valuation, a $20 billion chip startup—while in the market, Goldman reported that hedge funds have been quietly walking away from tech at a record pace. Between them sits Alibaba, previewing a 2.4-trillion-parameter model that makes China's frontier-model surge look less like a single Friday event than a campaign. On the positioning side, a reminder that navigation is now a weapon in both directions: Israel Aerospace Industries unveiled a system that stops drone swarms by taking their satellites away.

## Tech News

### Alibaba Previews Qwen3.8 Max, Claiming the Number-Two Spot Behind Fable 5

**Alibaba** previewed **Qwen3.8 Max**, its largest model ever at roughly **2.4 trillion** parameters, positioning it as second only to Anthropic's flagship **Fable 5**, per The Wall Street Journal. The preview is already live on Alibaba's coding platforms, with an open-weight release planned—meaning developers will be able to download and fine-tune a model the company is pitching as frontier-class.

The timing makes the pattern hard to miss: Qwen3.8 Max lands days after Moonshot's Kimi K3 and alongside Z.AI's GLM-5.2, three Chinese frontier-scale releases inside a single stretch of July, all of them heading toward open weights despite hardware constraints from US export controls. American labs have spent two years arguing that closed models justify their pricing through capability. Beijing's labs are now testing that argument at 2.4 trillion parameters, for free, at the developer's own download speed.

### Hedge Funds Have Dumped Tech at the Fastest Pace on Record, Goldman Says

Hedge funds pulled back from US technology stocks at a **record pace** over the past two months, per **Goldman Sachs**' Prime Services desk as reported by Bloomberg. The cohort was a net seller of the sector in **six of the past eight weeks**, with the cumulative reduction in market value totaling roughly **10%**—the largest retreat from tech since the data series began more than a decade ago.

Goldman notes the money is rotating into other sectors, consumer stocks among them, on concern that the largest technology companies could eventually throttle back their AI infrastructure spending. The pressure is showing up in names that led the melt-up, with Micron, SanDisk, and Alphabet among those weighed down. Bloomberg separately reports Big Tech now faces mounting pressure to justify its AI outlays as investors question near-term monetization—the same doubt from the other end of the telescope. The spending hasn't slowed; the willingness to underwrite it has.

### Hut 8 Signs a $9.8 Billion Lease That Fills a One-Gigawatt Texas Campus

**Hut 8** signed a second **15-year** lease worth **$9.8 billion** with an unnamed investment-grade customer, fully contracting its planned **one-gigawatt** Beacon Point data center campus in Texas, per Reuters. Shares rose in premarket trading; the deal follows an earlier lease at the same site and gives the former bitcoin miner long-term revenue visibility as it finishes building out the campus.

The transaction is a clean illustration of who actually owns the AI buildout's scarce inputs. Crypto miners spent years accumulating land, grid interconnects, power contracts, and cooling expertise—precisely the assets hyperscalers now cannot get fast enough. A single gigawatt campus draws power on the order of a major industrial plant, which is why 15-year contracts, not spot capacity, are how this market clears. The chips get the headlines; the electricity gets the lease.

## GNSS News

### IAI's HYPNOSIS Stops Drone Swarms by Taking Their Satellites Away

**Israel Aerospace Industries** unveiled **HYPNOSIS**, a high-power navigation warfare (NAVWAR) system built to defend fixed, high-value sites against GNSS-guided aerial threats—principally the large drone swarms that have defined recent conflicts—by attacking the satellite navigation link rather than the airframe, per Inside GNSS. The system arrays mobile jamming and spoofing stations around a protected site, each tied to a central command-and-control unit that handles detection, analysis, and response in real time.

The autonomy is the design point. IAI says the C2 architecture can operate without an operator directing individual stations, because a coordinated multi-directional attack can unfold in seconds—faster than a human can task emitters. Severing a drone's link to the constellation it navigates by is also dramatically cheaper than meeting it with an interceptor, which is the economic argument that has driven every counter-swarm program since cheap drones started outnumbering expensive missiles.

It is worth noting what this looks like from the civil side. The same month regulators and manufacturers have spent building GNSS resilience—authenticated signals, LEO backups, multi-source timing—a major defense supplier has productized deliberate, high-power, autonomous jamming and spoofing around protected sites. Both trends are rational, and they point at each other: the harder navigation warfare becomes as a defensive tool, the less any receiver anywhere can assume the signal it hears is the one that was sent.

## Key Takeaways

- **China's frontier-model surge is a campaign, not a moment**: Alibaba's 2.4-trillion-parameter Qwen3.8 Max, previewed with open weights planned, lands alongside Kimi K3 and GLM-5.2—three frontier-scale Chinese releases in July, all pressuring the premium that closed US models charge.
- **The market is selling the AI trade while the industry keeps buying it**: Goldman says hedge funds cut tech exposure about 10% in two months, the fastest on record, even as Hut 8 locked a $9.8 billion lease on a one-gigawatt campus and Databricks, Etched, and Bristol Myers wrote fresh checks for compute.
- **Navigation warfare is now a shipping product**: IAI's HYPNOSIS defends high-value sites by autonomously jamming and spoofing the GNSS link that drone swarms rely on—cheaper than interceptors, and a reminder that the civil resilience push and the military denial push are accelerating each other.
